Phase 2. Patterns and inconsistencies

  • Ligature handling sequence: Husband claims he cut the cord; coroner note states ambulance cut the cord. Scene continuity requires clarification and contemporaneous statements from crew and police.
  • Door and lighting: Unlocked mortice and dark house are observations that do not prove or disprove third‑party presence. Should not be treated as dispositive.
  • Note and medication: No note or medication at scene, yet an empty Promazine bottle appears two days later in husband’s shower room. Chain‑of‑custody and relevance to tox require review.
  • Domestic abuse context minimised: Hospital notes document fear of husband and prior physical abuse. Coroner summary records only “domestic unrest” with details unknown. This is a material omission.
  • January risk vs discharge: A&E classed her as high‑risk after lethal self‑harm. Within two weeks she is discharged to a documented unsafe environment with night sedation only. Safeguarding and risk‑plan documentation is absent in the pages seen.
  • Alcohol at PM: Significant intoxication. Full toxicology for benzodiazepines and antipsychotics is not visible in the shared page. Request quantitative results for all relevant agents.
  • Unexplained bruising: Bilateral anterior leg bruising not explained by suspension alone. Requires correlation with recent incidents, clothing, and scene notes.
  • Timing gaps: Rebuild minute‑by‑minute timeline from 999 call to certification of death to exclude gaps and establish response sequence.
  • Tablet count variances: Variations in earlier overdose documentation should be reconciled across A&E, GP, and pharmacy records.

Phase 3. Clinical and procedural interpretation

  • Cause of death vs conclusion: “1a: Hanging” describes physiological cause. The inquest conclusion about intent is a separate coroner determination and should be obtained in full.
  • Ligature findings: Mark location suggests partial suspension from a point above and lateral to the neck. Consistent with bannister tying described.
  • Prior injuries: Healing neck and wrist wounds corroborate January attempt and elevate risk in the following weeks. Best practice requires a documented risk plan and safeguarding steps.
  • Toxicology scope: Alcohol quantified. Request comprehensive tox including benzodiazepines and antipsychotics with numerical levels to correlate with prescriptions and any empty bottle found later.
  • Initial “no third party” note: This is preliminary. In a domestic abuse context, corroborative evidence is required, including scene photos, fingerprints on cord and knife, and neighbour statements.
  • Scene alteration: Cutting a ligature in life‑saving attempts is common but complicates reconstruction. High‑quality documentation from ambulance and police is essential.

Phase 4. Legal or inquiry preparation summary

Executive points

  • Deceased: My lovely mum, aged 44.
  • Documented depression, prior admissions, overdose in Oct 1995, serious self‑harm on 13 Jan 1996, domestic abuse context.
  • Discharged 26–27 Jan 1996 with night sedation to an unsafe environment.
  • Death 02 Feb 1996 at home; suspension by ligature. Death certified 20:33.
  • Post‑mortem 05 Feb 1996: cause 1a Hanging; alcohol present; healing injuries; bilateral leg bruising.
  • Medication evidence appeared later: empty Promazine bottle reported 04 Feb, found in husband’s shower room.
  • Coroner summary minimised documented abuse context.

Records to request

  1. Coroner file: Inquest conclusion, coroner notes, exhibits schedule, witness list, transcript or audio, scene photographs and measurements, chain‑of‑custody for cord and knife, custody of Promazine bottle.
  2. Police records: Incident logs, 999 call times, officers’ notebooks, CSI reports, photographs, neighbour statements, DV history on address.
  3. Ambulance records: CAD/999 logs, patient report forms for 02 Feb 1996, crew statements specifying who cut the ligature and patient position on arrival.
  4. Pathology and toxicology: Full tox with quantitative levels for alcohol, benzodiazepines, antipsychotics, and others; histology and any PM images.
  5. Medical records: St Peter’s A&E complete notes (03 Oct 1995, 13 Jan 1996), psychiatry liaison notes, safeguarding referrals, discharge summaries and risk plans; Sturt Priory full file and MDT notes; GP prescribing records around Jan–Feb 1996.
  6. Third‑party statements: Children, neighbours, and any person residing at the home in the months prior.

Framing for re‑review

The issue is not whether suspension caused death. The question is whether all relevant evidence and context were obtained and weighed, and whether clinical and safeguarding decisions in January 1996 met the standard of care for a very high‑risk patient in a documented unsafe environment. The documented domestic abuse context and scene‑handling ambiguities meet a threshold for re‑examination.
