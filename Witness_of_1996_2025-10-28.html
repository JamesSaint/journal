Witness of 1996

Written 2025-10-28

Introduction

I ran the coroner papers through a machine to see what I already knew. The output came back clean, aligned, and clinical. It mirrored the patterns I have carried in my body for nearly thirty years. It did not flinch. It did not soothe. It told the truth that the documents could bear and left the remaining weight with me.

This entry sits beside the factual matrix, not to restate it but to record the human impact. It is my witness statement as a son. It belongs next to the reconstruction I built: Coroner and Medical Record Reconstruction 1995–1996.

What the papers say and what my body knew

What the documents say What my body knew
Cause 1a recorded as hanging. Process notes, timings, inventories, signatures. Death did not start on that date. It started with a long accumulation of fear, silencing, and missed safeguarding. The moment was a culmination, not an isolated act.
“Domestic unrest” as a euphemism. Abuse context trimmed to fit the margins of a form. Unrest is a tidy word for terror. The house held dread. My nervous system kept those pages even when the file did not.
Discharge to home with night sedation two weeks after a high risk event. Sending someone back to the place that breaks them and calling it care is a wound that never learned to close.
Alcohol levels quantified. Medication references inconsistent across notes. Numbers can be precise and still miss the truth. The truth includes who poured, who minimized, who looked away.
Ligature cut by someone, sometime. Sequence in dispute. The sequence matters because it tells me whether anyone fully saw her. It also tells me why my body never stopped asking questions.

Meaning I am making now

I accept that the file will never hold everything. It can hold injuries, levels, and timings. It cannot hold the cost of being told to put a lid on the past. It cannot hold the way my mother’s absence kept rearranging the furniture of my life. It cannot hold the decades I spent wondering if I was allowed to trust my own perception.

What I can do is keep the factual record accurate and keep my witness alive. Both matter. The record honours reality. My witness restores dignity.

Integration

  • Hold the matrix and the memoir side by side. Neither cancels the other.
  • Ask for what was never given: full toxicology, full scene documentation, full safeguarding review. Not to punish, to complete the truth.
  • Let my body be a source of data. It has been reliable for years. I am allowed to trust it.
  • Grieve the home that was not safe and the care that was not care. Grief is not a failure. It is a correct response.
If authorities or clinicians wish to understand the human context behind the matrix, this page is my reference. It is not speculation. It is lived evidence.
